Methodological Definition: This site indexes public newspaper phrase appearances of the specific text string "hearing voices" from 1888 to the present. It tracks public language trends and archive density, not clinical or medical diagnoses.

Central Research Hypotheses

  • The UCA Hypothesis: Evaluating organized linguistic patterns via Unidentified Communication Agents as a working research model.
  • The Inside Out Process: Tracking how private, internal experiences are translated into formal institutional records.
  • Wartime Suppression: Analyzing how macro-geopolitical events (WWI and WWII) systematically displaced civilian phrase reporting in the press.

Historical Timeline & Trends

  • 1888: Established operational archive baseline based on press geographical distribution.
  • 1904–1912: Measurable surge in historical public reporting trends.
  • 1914–1918 & 1938–1944: Sharp declines correlating directly with major wartime media mobilization.
  • 1989–2002: Peak archive phrase density, followed by a steady macro decline.

Full Report: Hearing Voices, Public Reporting Patterns, War-Time Suppression, and the MARBLE / UCA Hypothesis

I. Executive Summary

This report organizes the Hearing Voices project into a historical, newspaper-archive, legal-medical, and threat-assessment framework.

The core dataset discussed in this project is not a clinical dataset of actual auditory hallucination events. It is a public-language dataset based on newspaper search results for the exact phrase “hearing voices.” Therefore, the most precise phrase throughout this report is: newspaper phrase appearances, not verified real-world voice-hearing incidents.

The working hypothesis preserved in this report is that MARBLE places the beginning of organized, human-sourced voice targeting around 1912, approximately 114 years before 2026. The alleged voices are identified by the project as UCAs — Unidentified Communication Agents — and are treated as a research hypothesis, not as a proven conclusion.

The major pattern observed is that the phrase “hearing voices” appears active in the early twentieth century, declines during major war periods, rises again during interwar or postwar civilian periods, does not show a clear public spike during the MKULTRA period, peaks in the mid-to-late 1990s, and then declines after approximately 1997–2002.

The decline continues through the methamphetamine epidemic period, which weakens the argument that methamphetamine alone explains the national newspaper phrase pattern.

II. Definitions

1. Hearing Voices

For this report, “hearing voices” refers to the exact newspaper phrase searched in newspaper archives. It may appear in psychiatric, legal, religious, fictional, political, public-health, or metaphorical contexts. Therefore, the phrase cannot be treated as a direct count of actual voice-hearing events.

2. UCAs — Unidentified Communication Agents

The MARBLE project uses UCA to mean Unidentified Communication Agent. This is the project’s label for the alleged source behind reported voices, especially voices described as commanding, coercive, humiliating, violent, or socially manipulative. The UCA theory remains a hypothesis.

3. Inside Out

Inside Out is defined as the alleged process by which a person’s internal life is provoked, mirrored, destabilized, observed, documented, and then converted into institutional evidence against that person.

Possible institutional forms include psychiatric labeling, criminal-risk documentation, credibility attack, court evidence, school-threat assessment, involuntary commitment screening, public shaming, digital record creation, and behavioral manipulation.

III. Central Research Questions

  • When did “hearing voices” become a recognizable public phrase?
  • Can a defensible baseline be created for newspaper phrase tracking?
  • Do major wars suppress or redirect public reporting of “hearing voices”?
  • Do drug epidemics explain the observed phrase pattern?
  • Does MKULTRA produce a visible public phrase effect?
  • Are modern psychiatric screens too shallow to detect command-voice risk?
  • Can school-violence prevention improve through deeper structured inquiry?

IV. Final Findings

Finding 1 — The dataset is phrase history, not incidence history. The graph tracks public newspaper appearances of “hearing voices.” It does not directly count actual voice-hearing events.

Finding 2 — War periods appear to suppress the phrase. The phrase declines during World War I and again during the World War II period. This supports a wartime-displacement theory.

Finding 3 — MKULTRA does not create a visible phrase spike. The MKULTRA years are historically important, but the exact phrase “hearing voices” does not visibly spike during the operational period.

Finding 4 — Drug epidemics do not explain the archive trend. Crack cocaine does not cleanly explain the 1990s pattern, and methamphetamine concern rises or persists while the phrase declines.

Finding 5 — Checkbox mental-health evaluation is inadequate. A yes/no question about voices without follow-up is a system failure. Command content, target fixation, weapon access, and ability to resist must be assessed.

V. Final Conclusion

The Hearing Voices project does not yet prove that UCAs are externally sourced human agents or that organized voice targeting began in 1912. However, the project has identified a meaningful public-language pattern: the phrase “hearing voices” was active before World War I, declined during major wars, returned during civilian periods, did not visibly spike during MKULTRA, peaked in the mid-to-late 1990s, and then declined through the methamphetamine epidemic era.

The strongest defensible conclusion is that the public record of “hearing voices” appears to be shaped less by actual incidence alone and more by war, media attention, psychiatric language, legal reporting, archive structure, drug-era narratives, and institutional framing.

The next research step is article-level sampling. The graph gives the pattern. The articles will give the meaning.
